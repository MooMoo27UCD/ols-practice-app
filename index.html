Case: Solar Panel Subsidy Program (100 points total)

Background

To promote renewable energy, the government launched a Solar Incentive Program that provides monetary subsidies to households installing rooftop solar panels.

Each region determines its own average subsidy amount, depending on local budgets and priorities.

An analyst collects data from 50 regions for one year:

  • Yi: Percentage of households that installed solar panels in region i (in percentage points).
  • Xi: Average subsidy amount (in hundreds of dollars) per eligible household in region i.

Q1. Understanding the Regression Model (15 pts)

Model: AdoptionRate = β₀ + β₁ × SubsidyAmount + u

  • Y (AdoptionRate): percentage of households adopting solar in a region (percentage points)
  • X (SubsidyAmount): average per-household subsidy in a region, measured in $100 units
  1. Explain in plain language what the model captures.
  2. Identify dependent and independent variables, and how they are measured.
  3. What does the error term u represent? Give two examples.
  4. Interpret β₀ and β₁ in context.
  5. What sign do you expect for β₁? Why?
  6. Does a positive slope imply causality? Briefly explain.
  • What it captures: Average change in solar adoption as subsidies vary across regions, holding other factors in expectation.
  • Variables: Y = adoption rate (% points). X = subsidy in $100 units.
  • Error term u: omitted drivers (e.g., sunlight/irradiance, electricity prices, income, environmental attitudes, installer capacity, permitting frictions, marketing).
  • β₀: baseline adoption when subsidy = 0. β₁: change in expected adoption (pp) per additional $100 subsidy.
  • Sign: β₁ > 0 expected (higher subsidies → higher adoption on average).
  • Association vs. causation: Positive slope ≠ causal; regions favoring solar may also fund higher subsidies.

Q2. Interpreting Estimated Coefficients (20 pts)

Estimated equation: AdoptionRate = 5.2 + 0.8 × SubsidyAmount

  1. Interpret the slope 0.8.
  2. What is the expected change if the average subsidy increases by $300?
  3. Interpret the intercept 5.2. Is it meaningful here?
  • Slope: Each additional $100 subsidy is associated with +0.8 percentage points in adoption.
  • $300 change: $300 = 3 × $100 ⇒ predicted change = 3 × 0.8 = +2.4 pp.
  • Intercept: Predicted adoption at subsidy = $0 (5.2 pp). Meaningful if zero-subsidy regions exist; otherwise an extrapolation to treat cautiously.

Q3. Assessing Model Fit (15 pts)

Reported R² = 0.45.

  1. Explain what R² = 0.45 means here.
  2. Is this a “good” fit? How should a manager interpret it?
  3. Can a model with modest R² still be useful? Why?
  • Meaning: 45% of cross-regional variation in adoption is explained by subsidy differences within this linear model.
  • Goodness: Moderate. Many other drivers matter (sunlight, income, installer capacity, permitting).
  • Usefulness: Yes—precisely estimated marginal effects can guide policy even when R² is modest.

Q4. Inference & Confidence Intervals (15 pts)

Given SE(β₁) = 0.3. Work at 95% confidence.

  1. Write the 95% CI formula for β₁.
  2. Without computing, describe what the interval means for policymakers.
  3. If the interval includes 0, what does that imply?
  4. If it excludes 0, what can we conclude about effectiveness?
  • Formula: β̂₁ ± 1.96 × SE(β̂₁).
  • Meaning: A plausible range for the true change in adoption (pp) per +$100 subsidy.
  • Includes 0: Insufficient evidence at 5% to claim a non-zero effect.
  • Excludes 0: Statistically significant evidence that subsidies affect adoption.

Q5. Hypothesis Testing & Decision-Making (20 pts)

Test H₀: β₁ = 0 vs. H₁: β₁ ≠ 0. Suppose p-value = 0.02.

  1. State the hypotheses in plain English.
  2. Interpret p = 0.02.
  3. Decision at α = 0.05.
  4. Define Type I and Type II errors for policymakers.
  5. Which error may be costlier here? Briefly justify.
  • H₀/H₁: H₀: subsidies have no linear effect. H₁: subsidies do affect adoption (increase or decrease).
  • p = 0.02: If the true effect were 0, seeing a slope at least this large would occur ≈2% of the time.
  • Decision: Reject H₀ at 5% (0.02 < 0.05).
  • Errors: Type I—conclude subsidies help when they don’t (waste funds). Type II—miss a real positive effect (lost decarbonization).
  • Costlier error: context-dependent: tight budgets → Type I; urgent climate targets → Type II.

Q6. Managerial & Policy Interpretation (15 pts)

Magnitude: β̂₁ = 0.8 pp per +$100 subsidy.

  1. Interpret this finding in plain language.
  2. Discuss whether the effect is economically meaningful given costs.
  3. How to weigh statistical vs practical significance?
  4. Write a 3–4 sentence executive summary advising the program direction.
  • Interpretation: +$100 average subsidy → ≈ +0.8 pp higher adoption.
  • Economic meaning: Compare cost per additional adopter with long-run benefits (emissions, resilience, bill savings).
  • Stat vs econ significance: Reliable ≠ important; assess magnitude vs goals and budget.
  • Executive summary (sample): Estimated relationship is positive and statistically significant. Effect size is moderate. Recommend maintaining the program and targeting increases where latent demand is high, while addressing non-price barriers (permitting, installer availability) to improve cost‑effectiveness. Continue monitoring outcomes to refine allocations.
